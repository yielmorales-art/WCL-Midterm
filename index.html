Session 5 — Affect and Poetry

Shikibu · Sarton · Alunan ▾

What is a Poem?

Poems use condensed, artistic language to express ideas, feelings, or stories. Unlike prose, poems rely on rhythm and sound as much as literal meaning.

Key Characteristics:

  • Structure: Written in lines (verses) grouped into stanzas.
  • Musicality: Uses meter and rhyme for a musical quality.
  • Imagery: Sensory language that "paints a picture."
  • Figurative Language: Metaphors, similes — create deeper meaning.

Defamiliarization (Ostranenie)

To take an ordinary experience and present it as strange, new, or unfamiliar — making the reader see it with fresh eyes.

Izumi Shikibu

"In this world, love has no color. / Yet, how deeply / my body is stained / by yours."

A Japanese poet of the Heian period. The poem uses defamiliarization to show love as an invisible yet deeply physical force.

Merlie Alunan — "We Kept a Jarful of Keys"

Known as the "Mother of Waray-waray Poetry"; advocates writing in Visayan mother tongues (Waray-waray, Cebuano). Her poem explores regret — how objects (keys) hold memory and loss.

May Sarton — "Autumn Sonnet"

What is a Sonnet?

  • Exactly 14 lines in iambic pentameter
  • Set rhyme scheme (varies by type)
  • Focuses on a single idea: love, beauty, time, mortality
  • Features a Volta (Turn) — a shift in tone or argument

The poem explores love entailing sacrifice. Aristotle argued the highest narrative form is tragedy — evoking pity and fear, providing catharsis. Does love that requires sacrifice become tragic?

Session 6 — Spaces of Terror & Sympathy

Wislawa Szymborska ▾

"Photograph from September 11" — Szymborska

"They jumped from the burning floors— / one, two, a few more, / higher, lower. / The photograph halted them in life, / and now keeps them / above the earth toward the earth... / I can do only two things for them— / describe this flight / and not add a last line."

Szymborska's poem responds to Richard Drew's "The Falling Man" photograph (Sept. 11, 2001). The poem refuses to give the subjects a "last line" — preserving them in suspended life rather than allowing death to define them.

Politics of Gaze

  • Positionality: Who is looking? From what vantage point? What is the object of inquiry?
  • Disasters and Sympathy: Who owns the story? Questions of subjectivity and vulnerability.
  • Solidarity and Empathy: Toward a more compassionate world.
  • Convergence of Arts: Photo|Graphs — art fossilized in space and time.

Literature of / in / as Photography

The session explores how literature and photography intersect: photography can be read as text, and literature can capture the stillness and immediacy of a photograph.

Historical Atrocity Images

The session also examined photographs of Philippine history — the First Battle of Bud Dajo (1906) and the Rape of Manila (WWII, 1945) — raising questions about who owns traumatic images and how the gaze of the conqueror frames the narrative of the conquered.

Central Question

"How do we measure the potency of words, of images — of art?"

Session 7 — Philippine Labour Migration

Merlinda Bobis ▾

Merlinda Bobis — Background

Filipino-Australian author whose multicultural heritage shapes her storytelling. Known for richly poetic prose and themes of migration, identity, displacement, and belonging.

"The Sadness Collector" — Plot & Context

Centers on Rica, a young girl whose mother has left the Philippines to work in Paris as a domestic helper (on a fake visa). The father struggles emotionally. The magical figure of Big Lady — a doll-like entity — absorbs the family's grief.

"A whisper of salt, even the smallest nudge to the palate, can betray a century of hidden grief... It is this residual contamination, our individual portions of grief, that she eats, so we do not die from them."

Narrative Techniques

  • Nonlinear Structure: Alternates past and present; reflects memory's cyclical nature.
  • Limited Third-Person POV: Close to Rica's internal feelings; creates intimacy.
  • Pacing: Balances high tension with calm; time shifts mark emotional journey.
  • Symbolism: Big Lady = emotional labor and the collective grief of the diaspora. The pink décor = "bravado" masking poverty and longing.

Major Themes

  • Migration & Diaspora: OFW experience; cultural displacement.
  • Family Separation: Emotional toll on children left behind.
  • Emotional Suppression: Characters suppress grief to survive.
  • Emotional Labor: The cost of maintaining cultural and personal connections across distance.

Session 12 — The Invention of the Sexual Self

Michel Foucault ▾

The "Commonsense" Story (That Foucault Challenges)

The popular belief: sex was open in the 17th century → Victorians made it taboo → we are now "liberating" ourselves. Foucault calls this the Repressive Hypothesis.

The Repressive Hypothesis

The belief that power's only relationship to sex is to say "No" — through laws, censorship, prohibitions. Foucault counters: if sex is so repressed, why has there been a massive "discursive explosion" (explosion of talk about sex) since the 1700s?

Power as Productive (Biopower)

  • Old View (Juridico-Discursive): Power = a limit, a prohibition.
  • New View (Biopower): Power produces life, health, and categories.
  • Power doesn't hide sex — it demands we find, name, and study it.

The Perverse Implantation

Before 18th century: "Sodomy" was a forbidden act (like theft). After 18th century: "The Homosexual" became a species — a personality type, an identity.

Four Strategic Directions of Power over Sex:

  • Hysterization: The female body defined by its reproductive system.
  • Pedagogization: Obsession with children's "secret" habits (masturbation).
  • Socialization: Using sex to manage birth rates and population.
  • Psychiatrization: Creation of clinical lists of "perversions."

Ars Erotica vs. Scientia Sexualis

  • Ars Erotica (Ancient/Eastern): Pleasure is a secret passed master to student. Goal: increase intensity of pleasure.
  • Scientia Sexualis (Modern/Western): Truth is found by confessing to an expert. Goal: reveal the "truth" of who you are.

The Spiral of Power and Pleasure

Talking about sex feels like freedom, but it binds us more tightly to medical and state categories. We are caught in a cycle — the doctor's pleasure in "finding" secrets, the patient's pleasure in "revealing" themselves.

"If the 'truth' of your soul is your sexuality, who gave you that truth?"
